The Daraa moment and the FSA emergence (March-December 2011)

[Source: UN Human Rights Council, 2011-08-18] In March 2011, protests begin in Daraa after the arrest and reported mistreatment of schoolboys accused of anti-government graffiti. Bashar al-Assad's government frames the unrest as a security challenge rather than a political opening. Local demonstrations spread as funerals become protest events and security responses harden. The escalation choice matters for extraction: the same actor, the Syrian state, moves from policing to coercive repression, and defections begin to turn protest into armed opposition. By late 2011, the Free Syrian Army appears as an umbrella label for defectors and local armed groups rather than a unified command.

Multilateralisation and the Geneva I communique (2012)

[Source: UN Action Group for Syria, 2012-06-30] The conflict becomes multilateral in 2012 as regional governments, the United Nations, and armed non-state actors increasingly shape the battlefield and diplomatic arena. Kofi Annan's six-point plan tries to freeze violence and open negotiations, but enforcement capacity remains weak. The Houla massacre in May 2012 intensifies international pressure and gives the conflict a sharper sectarian reading. Geneva I introduces the idea of a transitional governing body formed by mutual consent, a phrase that becomes a durable diplomatic constraint because parties interpret it differently.

The Ghouta chemical attack and the OPCW frame (August 2013)

[Source: OPCW-UN Joint Mission, 2013-09-27] The 2013-08-21 Ghouta chemical attack transforms the conflict from civil-war escalation into a norm-violation crisis. The United States had warned against chemical-weapons use, but the eventual Russian-brokered removal framework produces a paradox: a norm is affirmed through OPCW procedures while the coercive red line is not enforced through regime-change action. The extractor should capture Ghouta as an Event, Chemical Weapons Convention as a Norm, and the removal framework as a Process that changes later bargaining.

The rise of ISIS and the coalition response (2014)

[Source: UN Security Council, 2014-08-15] ISIS expands across eastern Syria and Iraq after the fall of Mosul in June 2014. The US-led coalition begins air operations against ISIS, while Kurdish forces become central ground partners. The Kurdish question introduces a durable contradiction: the United States gains an effective anti-ISIS partner in the YPG/SDF, while Turkey treats the same network as a security threat linked to the PKK. This creates overlapping allied and opposed edges that cannot be represented by a single camp label.

Russian intervention and Aleppo's fall (September 2015-December 2016)

[Source: UN OCHA, 2016-12-22] Russia enters militarily in September 2015, changing the regime's air-power, intelligence, and diplomatic shield. The intervention enables a sequence of offensives and culminates in the fall and evacuation of eastern Aleppo in December 2016. The causal chain is central to the demo: Russian air operations restore regime tempo; restored tempo enables siege warfare; Aleppo's fall damages opposition territorial coherence and changes diplomatic leverage.

Astana process and the frozen middle (2017-2019)

[Source: UN Special Envoy briefings, 2017-2019] The Astana process emerges in 2017 as Russia, Iran, and Turkey translate battlefield leverage into a forum that Geneva cannot supply. De-escalation zones codify asymmetric control rather than settlement. Turkey's Operation Peace Spring in 2019 further shows that Syria is not one conflict but a stack of linked theatres: regime-opposition, anti-ISIS, Turkish-Kurdish, Israeli-Iranian, and sanctions/reconstruction.

The COVID-19 plateau and the Captagon economy (2020-2023)

[Source: UN OCHA, 2023-12-31] Front lines plateau after 2020, but the conflict economy changes. Sanctions, reconstruction blockages, aid access, refugee fatigue, and the Captagon trade become structural mechanisms. The regime's survival is not equal to state recovery. The ontology should capture capital flows, compliance risk, illicit revenue, humanitarian access, and political normalization as separate objects.

The November 2024 HTS offensive and the December 8 collapse

[Source: TACITUS curated timeline, 2024-12-08] In late November 2024, HTS-led forces advance from Aleppo toward Hama, Homs, and Damascus in a rapid sequence. Assad's flight to Moscow and the collapse of regime command structures raise a classification problem: this is not a negotiated settlement and not a mutual Glasl stage-9 abyss. It is a structural-collapse event shaped by weakened external scaffolding, exhausted state institutions, and opportunistic successor governance. The post-collapse question is recurrence risk, not only reconstruction.
